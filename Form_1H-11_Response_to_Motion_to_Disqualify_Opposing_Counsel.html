C. Argument & Authorities

{CHOOSE APPROPRIATE PARAGRAPH 6}

6. A lawyer or law firm should not be disqualified from representing a party in a matter adverse to a former client unless the matter is “substantially related” to the earlier representation. Tex. Disc. R. Prof. Conduct 1.09; In re Epic Holdings, Inc., 985 S.W.2d 41, 43 (Tex. 1998); Texaco, Inc. v. Garcia, 891 S.W.2d 255, 256 (Tex. 1995). The substantial relationship test requires proof that the facts of the earlier representation are so related to the facts in the pending litigation that a genuine threat exists that confidences revealed to former counsel will be divulged to a present adversary. Metropolitan Life Ins. Co. v. Syntek Fin. Corp., 881 S.W.2d 319, 320-21 (Tex. 1994). {See O’Connor’s Texas Rules, “Lawyer represented other party,” ch. 1-H, §8.2.2, p. 45.}

6. If a lawyer becomes a witness, the lawyer is disqualified to act as a lawyer in the case. Mauze v. Curry, 861 S.W.2d 869, 870 (Tex. 1993). If a lawyer’s testimony may be necessary to establish an essential fact on his client’s behalf, the court should disqualify the lawyer. See In re Bahn, 13 S.W.3d 865, 874 (Tex. App.—Fort Worth 2000, orig. proceeding); Warrilow v. Norrell, 791 S.W.2d 515, 523 n.10 (Tex. App.—Corpus Christi 1989, writ denied). If the lawyer’s role as both an advocate and a witness will cause the opposing party actual prejudice, the court should disqualify the lawyer. In re Sanders, 153 S.W.3d 54, 57 (Tex. 2004); Ayres v. Canales, 790 S.W.2d 554, 558 (Tex. 1990). A lawyer who testifies as a fact witness may, however, continue representation if the attorney’s withdrawal would work a substantial hardship on the client because of the distinctive value of the lawyer as counsel in the particular case. Ayres, 790 S.W.2d at 557; Warrilow, 791 S.W.2d at 520. {See O’Connor’s Texas Rules, “Lawyer as witness,” ch. 1-H, §8.2.5, p. 46.}
