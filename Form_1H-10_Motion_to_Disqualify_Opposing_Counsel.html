{CHOOSE APPROPRIATE PARAGRAPH 6}

6. A lawyer or law firm cannot represent a party in a matter adverse to a former client-unless the client consents-if the matter is “substantially related” to the earlier representation. Tex. Disc. R. Prof. Conduct 1.09(a); In re Epic Holdings, Inc., 985 S.W.2d 41, 43 (Tex. 1998); Texaco, Inc. v. Garcia, 891 S.W.2d 255, 256 (Tex. 1995). The substantial relationship test requires proof that the facts of the earlier representation are so related to the facts in the pending litigation that there is a genuine threat that confidences revealed to former counsel will be divulged to a present adversary. Metropolitan Life Ins. Co. v. Syntek Fin. Corp., 881 S.W.2d 319, 320-21 (Tex. 1994). {See O’Connor’s Texas Rules, “Lawyer represented other party,” ch. 1-H, §8.2.2, p. 45.}

6. A firm must be disqualified if a partner or associate of the firm previously represented an adverse party in a matter “substantially related” to the pending litigation. See Texaco, Inc. v. Garcia, 891 S.W.2d 255, 256-57 (Tex. 1995); Henderson v. Floyd, 891 S.W.2d 252, 253-54 (Tex. 1995). The substantial relationship test requires proof that the facts of the earlier representation are so related to the facts in the pending litigation that there is a genuine threat that confidences revealed to former counsel will be divulged to a present adversary. Metropolitan Life Ins. Co. v. Syntek Fin. Corp., 881 S.W.2d 319, 320-21 (Tex. 1994). {See O’Connor’s Texas Rules, “Lawyer represented other party,” ch. 1-H, §8.2.2(1)-(2), p. 45.}

6. A firm must be disqualified if one of its employees who works on the lawsuit previously worked on the same lawsuit at another firm representing the adversary. See In re American Home Prods. Corp., 985 S.W.2d 68, 74 (Tex. 1998); Grant v. Thirteenth Ct. of Appeals, 888 S.W.2d 466, 467-68 (Tex. 1994). There is a conclusive presumption that confidences were revealed during the course of the employee’s prior employment. See Phoenix Founders, Inc. v. Marshall, 887 S.W.2d 831, 834 (Tex. 1994). {See O’Connor’s Texas Rules, “Nonlawyer employee,” ch. 1-H, §8.2.2(3), p. 45.}

6. If a lawyer becomes a witness, the lawyer is disqualified to act as a lawyer in the case. Mauze v. Curry, 861 S.W.2d 869, 870 (Tex. 1993). When a lawyer’s testimony may be necessary to establish an essential fact on its client’s behalf, the court should disqualify the lawyer. See In re Bahn, 13 S.W.3d 865, 874 (Tex. App.—Fort Worth 2000, orig. proceeding); Warrilow v. Norrell, 791 S.W.2d 515, 523 n.10 (Tex. App.— Corpus Christi 1989, writ denied). If the lawyer’s role as both an advocate and a witness will cause the opposing party actual prejudice, the court should disqualify the lawyer. In re Sanders, 153 S.W.3d 54, 57 (Tex. 2004); Ayres v. Canales, 790 S.W.2d 554, 558 (Tex. 1990). {See O’Connor’s Texas Rules, “Lawyer as witness,” ch. 1-H, §8.2.5, p. 46.}
