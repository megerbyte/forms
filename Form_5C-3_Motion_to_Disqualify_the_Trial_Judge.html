4. Under Texas Constitution article 5, section 11, a judge is disqualified from sitting in judgment when

{CHOOSE APPROPRIATE GROUND}

(A) the judge served as an attorney in the matter in controversy. Tex. Const. art. 5, §11. A judge is prohibited by the Texas Constitution from sitting in any case where {he/she} has been counsel. Tex. Const. art. 5, §11; Slaven v. Wheeler, 58 Tex. 23, 26 (1882); Williams v. Kirven, 532 S.W.2d 159, 161 (Tex. App.—Austin 1976, writ ref’d n.r.e.); cf. Greer v. State, 999 S.W.2d 484, 487-88 (Tex. App.—Houston [14th Dist.] 1999, pet. ref’d) (criminal case). {See O’Connor’s Texas Appeals, “Judge served as a lawyer,” ch. 13-J, §3.1, p. 104.}

(B) the case is one in which the judge “may be interested.” Tex. Const. art. 5, §11; Cameron v. Greenhill, 582 S.W.2d 775, 776 (Tex. 1979). The interest must be a direct pecuniary or personal interest in the result of the case. Cameron, 582 S.W.2d at 776. Once an interest is established, the judge is disqualified no matter how slight the interest. Id. {See O’Connor’s Texas Appeals, “Disqualifying interests,” ch. 13-J, §3.2, p. 105.}

(C) the judge is related to a party in the suit within the third degree of affinity or consanguinity. See Tex. Const. art. 5, §11. The Constitution does not identify the degree of relationship that will result in disqualification; it merely states a judge shall not sit in a case where related to a party “within such a degree as may be prescribed by law.” Id. Texas Rule of Civil Procedure 18b(1)(c) defines the prohibited degrees of relationship for disqualification. {See O’Connor’s Texas Appeals, “Related to party,” ch. 13-J, §3.3, p. 106.}

4. Under Texas Rule of Civil Procedure 18b(1), a judge is disqualified from sitting in judgment when

{CHOOSE APPROPRIATE GROUND}

(A) the judge served as an attorney in the matter in controversy. Tex. R. Civ. P. 18b(1)(a). {See O’Connor’s Texas Appeals, “Association with lawyer,” ch. 13-J, §3.4.1(1), p. 108.}

(B) an attorney, who previously practiced law with the judge, served as an attorney in the matter in controversy during the association. Tex. R. Civ. P. 18b(1)(a); In re O’Connor, 92 S.W.3d 446, 449 (Tex. 2002). {See O’Connor’s Texas Appeals, “Association with lawyer,” ch. 13-J, §3.4.1(1), p. 108.}

(C) the judge knows that, individually or as a fiduciary, {he/she} has an interest in the subject matter in controversy. Tex. R. Civ. P. 18b(1)(b). {See O’Connor’s Texas Appeals, “Interest in suit,” ch. 13-J, §3.4.1(2), p. 108.}

(D) the judge is related by affinity or consanguinity to one of the parties in the lawsuit within the third degree. Tex. R. Civ. P. 18b(1)(c). {See O’Connor’s Texas Appeals, “Related to party,” ch. 13-J, §3.4.1(3), p. 108.}
